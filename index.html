Mary Rose

An Interactive Essay

Mary Rose is a digital story crafted into an electronic literature piece by Natasha Nunn, initially intended to be a novel. This work emerged from a Digital Fictions class project in 2019, and it's a narrative about Nunn’s children’s great-grandmother, evolving from a family tale into an interactive digital experience (Nunn, 2019). As a piece of e-lit, it allows readers to engage physically with the story through scrolling and clicking.
The interactivity in Mary Rose is both intuitive and essential. The text is enveloped in fog-like graphics, with arrows guiding the reader to scroll down. This act reveals more of the story and invites readers to interact with dots that, when clicked, display new images and narratives. These dots are crucial in determining how the story unfolds for the reader, although there only is one “ending”. In conclusion, a choice between two options - “Am I still needed / or is it time to go?” - affects the visual representation of the ending, one shrouded in black, the other submerged underwater, shows they can choose what they want, but it all leads to the end.
Defining Mary Rose in terms of genre, it fits comfortably within the definitions of interactive fiction and digital storytelling. It could also be described as a digital dramatised memoir, considering its story based on personal history, combining factual and fictional elements. The narrative carries ghost story features, focusing on memories and the past.
Nonlinearity is a prominent aspect of the experience. The reader’s journey through the story differs by the sequence in which they interact with the dots, introducing a non-prescribed path through the narrative. This format suggests elements of aleatory processes, where the reader's actions - clicking and scrolling - introduce a sense of randomness to the unfolding of the tale.
Our analysis incorporates the distinctive use of clouds that is used as background and frame in Mary Rose, which we felt were central visuals to the story. We reflected on this technique in our work, building on its simplicity and thematic impact. Our version borrows the process of uncovering hidden thoughts or memories, a nod to Mary Roses’ design by having text hide behind these clouds and only shown through interacting. Mary Rose has a rather muted colour palette, we wanted our presentation to be more vibrant. In conclusion, Mary Rose is an exemplary work of e-lit, using digitality to add depth through visuals, immersion and interactivity to a story that could have been a traditional novel. Mary Rose exemplifies how a story can be enhanced and get a new and deeper meaning through digital elements.
